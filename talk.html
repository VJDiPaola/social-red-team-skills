Red-team your
good ideas.

How to use AI as the skeptic you should have met before you started.

Vince DiPaola teamvince.com

Tell five friends you want to start a community fridge network.

Now imagine you told them you wanted to launch a fintech.

Friends · the fridge pitch

"That's beautiful."

"How can I help?"

"My cousin would love this."

Friends · the fintech pitch

"What's your moat?"

"Have you talked to compliance?"

"How are you not Plaid?"

Both ideas can fail. One gets stress-tested at the start. The other gets stress-tested at month nine when the volunteers burn out and the local mutual aid group quietly stops returning your calls.

Nobody wants to be the person poking holes in a good cause.

That politeness has a price. It just gets paid later, by the founders, the volunteers, and the people the project was supposed to serve.

Mission-driven ideas get softer feedback than commercial ones. They need harder feedback, not less.

The intent is good. So the conversation tilts toward encouragement, away from the load-bearing questions. The result is a project that survives the brunch test and dies in production.

AI doesn't care about being polite.

Two skill files, copy and paste. Both are designed to be uncomfortable. That is the feature.

Skill 1 · Sparring partner · before you build

The hole you wouldn't find on your own.

Five pressure points. The hardest is usually the second one.

You
Want to start a network of community fridges across Brooklyn. Stocked by neighbors, free for anyone, mapped on a website.
Sparring partner
Pressure point 2 of 5: Who's not in the room.Name the last actual conversation you had with someone who relies on free community food. First name, roughly when. Not about your fridge idea. About their week.
Sparring partner
Pressure point 4: The boring reason it doesn't exist yet.The Friendly Fridge in the Bronx. In Our Hearts NYC. The original Love Fridge in Chicago. Some thrived. Many quietly closed. Before you place a single fridge, what specifically did the ones that stalled run out of: volunteers, electricity payments, food donations, or landlord patience?
Sparring partner
Pressure point 5: The ugly version of success.You hit 50 fridges. Local press loves it. The existing mutual aid group running 3 fridges can't recruit volunteers anymore, because everyone joined yours. You absorbed their work without their wisdom. Is that a win?
Skill 2 · Pre-mortem · before you launch

The funeral you hold before the launch.

Time-shift to the future where the project failed. Then make the user explain why.

Pre-mortem
Step 1: Time-shift.It is 18 months from today. The fridge network is dead. Not struggling, dead. You shut it down last week. A friend asks what happened. What do you tell them?
You
Honestly, the host businesses kept pulling the plug. Three got tired of the foot traffic by month four. We couldn't replace them fast enough. Then a Department of Health inspector flagged us in month six and we never recovered.
Pre-mortem
Step 4: Tripwire.Top failure mode is host pullout. What signal in month 3 would tell you this is already happening? Not "vibes seem off." A number.

Pre-commit: if you lose 2 of your first 5 hosts by month 3, you stop adding fridges and triage the host model.
Pre-mortem
Step 6: Calibration.On a 1 to 5, how committed are you now compared to before we started?

You: 3, was 5. Less excited. More ready.

One discipline. Two stages.

Stage 1 · Before you build

Sparring partner

Pressure-test the idea. Find the load-bearing assumption. Make yourself defend it with evidence.

Stage 2 · Before you launch

Pre-mortem

Imagine the project failed. Work backward to causes of death. Set a tripwire for each.

Together they cost an afternoon. The wrong project costs six months.
